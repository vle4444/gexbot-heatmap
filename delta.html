GEXBOT · HEATMAP Δ — CONTROL REFERENCE

Live options-flow heatmap. Each column is a snapshot; each row is a strike (or offset from spot). Color shows the strike's raw GEX or change, normalized by the current Color-scale mode.

Measure

Chooses what the cell value is.

Raw GEX
Each cell is the current GEX imbalance at that strike. Shows where positioning currently sits.
Δ Change
Each cell is the current GEX minus the GEX from N snapshots ago (Window setting). Shows inflow/outflow in real time.
Use Raw for static positioning context. Use Δ for flow detection — watching dealers accumulate or shed exposure as the session progresses.

Window (Δ mode only)

Lookback distance for the Δ subtraction. Smaller = more reactive, noisier. Larger = smoother, slower to react. 10 snaps ≈ 10 seconds at the default 1s refresh.

Y-axis

Absolute price
Y-axis is strike price. Traditional. Walls sit at their actual prices; spot trace animates across time.
Offset (pts)
Y-axis is strike − spot. Walls "lock" to their offset from spot; a wall 20 points above spot stays at +20 even as spot drifts.
Offset (%)
Same idea, but percentage-based. Useful when comparing across instruments or across wide price ranges.
When spot moves more than a few strikes worth during the session, offset modes keep walls visually aligned so you can see them persisting even as the chart scrolls vertically.

Color scale

Every cell is colored based on value ÷ denominator. The scale mode controls what that denominator is — and whether it's computed globally across the view (so the past repaints when it changes) or per-column and frozen at paint time.

Unified scale — past repaints

All cells share one denominator. When the denominator changes (new max, or you scroll), every cell on screen re-normalizes and visibly restretches.

Window: last 1 / 5 / 15 min
Single max computed over the trailing N minutes of data, applied uniformly to the whole visible range. A new large value pushes the denominator up and fades older cells.
Window: visible range
Single max over whatever is currently on screen. Scrolling or zooming changes the scope, hence the max, hence all colors.

Per-column, frozen at paint time

Each column uses its own denominator computed from data available at or before that column's timestamp. Once a column is drawn, its colors never change.

Trailing max: last 1 / 5 / 15 min
Each snapshot normalized against its own trailing N-minute max. Live-edge still twitches as new data arrives, but history is locked.
Snapshot only
Each column self-normalizes to its own strike max. Every column reads as "fully saturated." Loses cross-time magnitude comparison but shows each snapshot's internal shape with maximum clarity.
Cumulative (session anchored)
Running max from the earliest visible snapshot to each column. Monotonic non-decreasing — past colors can only ever dim, never brighten, and only when a new record appears.

Experimental

EMA (smoothed trailing, α=0.1)
Exponentially-weighted moving average of per-snapshot max, then each column uses its EMA value. Smooths out the twitching that trailing-max modes have. Good for cleaner visuals without the repaint behavior of Window modes.
Trailing p95 (5 min)
95th percentile of |values| over trailing 5 min, instead of max. Ignores single-snapshot outlier spikes that would otherwise dominate the scale. Makes the "typical" activity more legible.
Log-compressed (visible range)
Max over visible range, but values pass through sign(v) · log(1+|v|) / log(1+max) before mapping to color. Compresses the top end and expands the bottom end — useful when one huge wall would otherwise wash everything else out.
Z-score per snapshot (±3σ)
Cell colored by (v − mean) / (3 × std) within each snapshot, clipped to ±1. Highlights per-snapshot outliers — the "unusual" strikes relative to the rest of that moment's distribution. Absolute magnitude is lost; you're seeing statistical salience.

Manual

Fixed cap
You type the denominator (Cap field appears when selected). Never changes — good for A/B comparisons across sessions.

Palette

Color scheme for positive/negative values. GEX (green/red) is the convention; Electric, Solar, and Ice/Fire are aesthetic alternatives using equally-saturated complementary hues with black at zero.

Hi-contrast (white-tipped)
Both poles ramp toward near-white at peak. Peaks hit the highest luminance of any palette here, so big walls "burn brighter" against the black background and changes in the largest values are easier to spot. Sign carried by tint at sub-peak: yellow-white for +, pink-white for −.
Stepped (4 bands)
Quantizes |t| into 4 brightness bands (0.45, 0.65, 0.85, 1.0). Any non-zero value snaps to ≥45% brightness, so a wall popping into existence is loud rather than a soft fade-in. A wall crossing a band boundary produces a visible step, not a smooth shade, making magnitude changes easy to spot. Lime + / hot magenta −. Magnitude resolution is intentionally coarse — pair with a Trailing or Snapshot scale if you want band transitions to track local activity.

Gamma

Non-linearity of the color mapping. Controls whether brightness reflects value linearly or is compressed for perceptual lift.

Linear (1.0)
True proportional rendering. A cell at 50% of max is exactly half as bright as full saturation. Most honest, but near-zero cells look close to black.
0.7
Mild brightness lift for low values without destroying magnitude contrast.
0.55 (old)
Original behavior. Heavily compresses mid-to-high and lifts low values — punchy visual but flattens differences between large magnitudes.

Blend

How each strike fills its vertical pixel footprint in the heatmap.

None (sharp)
Flat rectangles per strike. No interpolation between strikes. Maximum faithfulness; shows each strike as an isolated cell.
Additive
Each strike adds a Gaussian brightness contribution. Soft and glow-like; same-sign clusters reinforce into bright peaks. Can wash out small opposite-sign strikes wedged between large same-sign neighbors.
Max (sign-safe)
Winner-take-all per pixel. Each strike holds the pixels where its own weighted contribution is largest. Sign identity is preserved; opposite-sign neighbors cannot cancel small strikes.

Col px

Pixel width of each snapshot column. Higher = larger cells, fewer snapshots on screen. Shift+scroll over the heatmap does the same thing live.

2 / 4 / 6 / 8 / 12
Standard widths — one snapshot per column.
1 / 0.5 / 0.25 / 0.1
Sub-pixel widths. Multiple snapshots collapse into a single output column via max-abs per strike — the strongest signal at each strike per time-bin survives. Lets you see longer time ranges at the cost of within-bin temporal resolution.
1d
Fixed layout for exactly one full US regular trading session (6.5h at 1s polling = 23,400 snaps). The width never changes as the buffer grows — the rendered session fills more of the canvas as the day progresses. Useful for "always-on session view."
fit
Auto-sizes to fit the entire current buffer in one frame, whatever its length. Aggregation kicks in automatically as the buffer grows.

Strike px

Cap each strike's vertical band height in CSS pixels. Controls how cramped or spread-out the heatmap looks vertically.

fit
Each strike fills the full slot between itself and the next strike. The traditional view — continuous heat field, no gaps. Bands grow taller as you zoom in vertically.
2 / 3 / 4 / 6 / 8 / 12
Fixed band thickness regardless of zoom. Black space appears between strikes — a Bookmap-style ladder where every strike reads as its own discrete level. Smaller numbers = more separation.
The Gaussian blend kernel scales with this setting. A thin Strike px gets a tiny kernel, so even in Additive or Max blend mode, neighboring strikes won't bleed into each other.

Refresh

How often the browser polls the API. 1s is fine for State-tier data; lower-frequency if you want to reduce load.

Session Save / Load / Restore

SAVE
Downloads the current snapshot buffer as a JSON file (gexbot-TICKER-TIMESTAMP.json). Includes ticker, expiry, overlay, and all snapshots in memory.
LOAD
Opens a file picker, loads a previously saved session JSON. Pauses live polling, switches Ticker/Expiry/Overlay to match the file, replaces the buffer. Click LIVE to resume appending new snapshots from where the file ended.
RESTORE
Auto-saved local backup. Every snapshot is written to the browser's IndexedDB in the background, keyed by date + ticker + expiry + overlay. After a server restart or browser reload, this button re-enables itself with the count for today's session matching your current selection — click to repopulate the buffer. Sessions older than today are auto-purged on startup. Browser-local: closing the browser profile or clearing site data wipes it. Use SAVE for portable archives.
LOAD and RESTORE both replace the current buffer — SAVE first if you want a portable copy. All MaxCh detection modes (CUSUM presets and event detectors) work on restored data.

Level traces

M+ (green)
Strike with the largest positive GEX imbalance at each snapshot.
M− (red)
Strike with the largest negative GEX imbalance.
ZG (yellow dashed)
Zero-gamma line. Only populated in GEX overlay mode (Greek overlays do not return zero_gamma).

Each is drawn as a horizontal trace across the visible columns. When the level jumps between strikes mid-session, the run at the old level ends and a new one starts at the new level — there's no vertical connector drawn through the intermediate prices.

MaxCh overlay

The MaxCh selector overlays strikes that are absorbing sustained GEX-imbalance flow, sourced from the API's /maxchange endpoint (works in every overlay mode, Greek included). The raw feed is noisy — six buckets each pick a winning strike every second, with adjacent strikes randomly trading places. The filter applies a per-strike CUSUM persistence test with hysteresis on top, so glyphs only appear once a strike has been firing across multiple buckets for several seconds, and only disappear once activity drops well below the entry threshold.

off
No overlay.
loose
Fast trigger, more events shown. Use when the market is quiet and you want to see anything moving. (k=1.5, hhigh=4, hlow=2)
normal — default
Balanced — agg-mode-like density but stable across snapshots. (k=1.5, hhigh=6, hlow=3)
strict
Only the strongest sustained events survive. Use during heavy flow when normal is still busy. (k=1.5, hhigh=10, hlow=5)
tight
~1.5× strict's threshold. Same reference value, but a strike must accumulate longer before it lights up. (k=1.5, hhigh=15, hlow=8)
severe
Both knobs raised: higher reference value (k=2.0) means small single-bucket prints decay faster than they accumulate, on top of a much higher threshold. Lone-bucket sporadic winners are effectively suppressed. (k=2.0, hhigh=22, hlow=11)
extreme
Only the most persistent multi-bucket events survive — minutes of sustained 3+-bucket dominance at the same strike. Most sessions will show very few glyphs. (k=2.5, hhigh=35, hlow=18)

The added tight / severe / extreme tiers extend the same CUSUM + hysteresis machinery, escalating h (decision threshold — primary knob, ARL₀ grows ~exponentially in h) and then k (reference value, raises the noise-floor each accumulator must clear per snapshot). Use when even strict is producing too many glyphs.

Event detectors — burst / swarm / pump

The CUSUM presets above are persistence detectors — they require multi-second sustained activity before lighting up, which by design filters out fast events like pumps. The event detectors below complement that by firing on sudden coordinated activity and staying quiet in steady state.

burst
Velocity z-score across all firing strikes. Sums |dir| over each snapshot, tracks a sliding 60-snap mean and σ, fires when the current snap's total exceeds mean + 2.5σ. Glyphs render at strikes contributing ≥ 15% of the spike's total magnitude. Catches general activity surges anywhere in the chain. Quiet because it normalizes against recent baseline.
swarm
Cluster detector. Fires when ≥ 2 same-sign firing strikes sit within ±5 price points of each other in the same snapshot, each carrying ≥ 5% of session max-abs. Catches "sweep" patterns where flow lights up a row of adjacent strikes simultaneously — signature of aggressive directional trading. Quiet because random single-strike noise rarely clusters.
pump — most stringent
Strict combination: burst condition (z ≥ 2.0, lowered slightly since combined) AND swarm condition AND the cluster center is within ±0.5% of latest spot. Designed for genuine pump precursors — coordinated near-spot directional surges. Will fire least often; expect long quiet periods punctuated by a few clean events per session.

Event detectors and CUSUM presets answer different questions. Use a CUSUM preset for "where is flow building over time"; use an event detector for "is something happening right now." They're mutually exclusive in the dropdown — pick one based on what you're watching for.

Glyph encoding: color = direction of imbalance change (green = call-side accumulation, red = put-side accumulation); size = magnitude of the most recent print at the strike, normalized over the visible window; opacity = same.

Reading the layer: a band of glyphs at one strike across many consecutive snapshots = real persistent flow. The hysteresis means the strike won't drop in/out frame-by-frame at the threshold boundary — it stays "on" once it crosses the entry bar, until activity decays well below it. Solid clusters lining up vertically with the brightest heatmap bands are the "where the action is" strikes.

Theory note: the CUSUM detector (Page 1954) is minimax-optimal for change detection subject to a false-alarm bound (Lorden 1971); hysteresis (Schmitt 1938) is the canonical display-layer debouncer. Picked over Bayesian online changepoint detection (Adams & MacKay 2007) and BVP-style multi-stream detectors (Xie & Siegmund 2013) for simplicity — both available as upgrades if needed. Wavelets and microstructure-noise filters (Aït-Sahalia / Mykland / Zhang) and VPIN solve adjacent but distinct problems. The event detectors are simpler statistics (running z-score, cluster count) chosen for responsiveness rather than minimax optimality.
