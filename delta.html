GEXBOT · HEATMAP Δ — CONTROL REFERENCE

Live options-flow heatmap. Each column is a snapshot; each row is a strike (or offset from spot). Color shows the strike's raw GEX or change, normalized by the current Color-scale mode.

Crosshair & tooltip

Hover anywhere in the chart area to see a vertical column-aligned line, a horizontal cursor-tracking line, and a floating tooltip with timestamp, the y-value at the cursor, the nearest strike + its raw value, and the snapshot's spot / zero-γ / net-GEX. The tooltip auto-flips to the cursor's other side near the right and bottom edges.

Mark (annotations)

Persistent markup layer for journaling key levels and times. Saved to the browser's IndexedDB, scoped to (ticker, expiry, overlay), and survives reloads. Three types selectable in the Mark dropdown:

price line
Click anywhere in the chart → drops a horizontal dashed line at that absolute price. Renders correctly across Y-axis modes (absolute, offset pts, offset %) — the price is preserved, the y-position adapts.
time marker
Click → vertical dashed line at the snapshot under the cursor. Useful for marking releases (CPI, FOMC, options expiration close).
point + note
Click → small dot at (price, time) with a free-text label. Use for one-off observations ("first 0DTE wall", "M+ flipped").

Right-click any annotation (within ~8 px) to delete it. The ⌫ button next to the dropdown clears all annotations for the current ticker/expiry/overlay.

Replay

The REPLAY button opens a scrubber bar across the bottom of the dashboard. Live polling pauses; you can:

▶ / ❚❚
Play / pause the playhead. Speed selector picks snapshots-per-second (1× = real-time, 60× = one minute of session per second).
Drag the knob
Scrub to any point in the buffer. Click the track to jump.
EXIT
Leave replay; live polling resumes from where you paused, jumping back to the live edge.

Combine with the IDB-restored sessions (RESTORE) to replay an entire trading day at 30× and study how walls formed and broke. This is the fastest way to build pattern recognition for option flow.

Regime badge

The Regime pill in the stats bar classifies the current GEX environment:

Long γ · fade
Spot is above zero gamma. Dealers are net long γ → they hedge by buying dips and selling rips → mean-reverting flow. Walls magnetize, breakouts often fail.
Short γ · chase
Spot is below zero gamma. Dealers are net short γ → they hedge by selling dips and buying rips → trend-amplifying flow. Walls become barriers; breakouts accelerate.
Flip · ZG ±X%
Spot is within ±5 bp of zero gamma — transitional zone, neither regime is committed. Treat carefully.
The regime tells you how to interpret every other signal on screen. A wall in long-γ vs short-γ behaves differently — same data, different read.

Measure

Chooses what the cell value is.

Raw GEX
Each cell is the current GEX imbalance at that strike. Shows where positioning currently sits.
Δ Change
Each cell is the current GEX minus the GEX from N snapshots ago (Window setting). Shows inflow/outflow in real time.
Use Raw for static positioning context. Use Δ for flow detection — watching dealers accumulate or shed exposure as the session progresses.

Window (Δ mode only)

Lookback distance for the Δ subtraction. Smaller = more reactive, noisier. Larger = smoother, slower to react. 10 snaps ≈ 10 seconds at the default 1s refresh.

Y-axis

Absolute price
Y-axis is strike price. Traditional. Walls sit at their actual prices; spot trace animates across time.
Offset (pts)
Y-axis is strike − spot. Walls "lock" to their offset from spot; a wall 20 points above spot stays at +20 even as spot drifts.
Offset (%)
Same idea, but percentage-based. Useful when comparing across instruments or across wide price ranges.
When spot moves more than a few strikes worth during the session, offset modes keep walls visually aligned so you can see them persisting even as the chart scrolls vertically.

Color scale

Every cell is colored based on value ÷ denominator. The scale mode controls what that denominator is — and whether it's computed globally across the view (so the past repaints when it changes) or per-column and frozen at paint time.

Unified scale — past repaints

All cells share one denominator. When the denominator changes (new max, or you scroll), every cell on screen re-normalizes and visibly restretches.

Window: last 1 / 5 / 15 min
Single max computed over the trailing N minutes of data, applied uniformly to the whole visible range. A new large value pushes the denominator up and fades older cells.
Window: visible range
Single max over whatever is currently on screen. Scrolling or zooming changes the scope, hence the max, hence all colors.

Per-column, frozen at paint time

Each column uses its own denominator computed from data available at or before that column's timestamp. Once a column is drawn, its colors never change.

Trailing max: last 1 / 5 / 15 min
Each snapshot normalized against its own trailing N-minute max. Live-edge still twitches as new data arrives, but history is locked.
Snapshot only
Each column self-normalizes to its own strike max. Every column reads as "fully saturated." Loses cross-time magnitude comparison but shows each snapshot's internal shape with maximum clarity.
Cumulative (session anchored)
Running max from the earliest visible snapshot to each column. Monotonic non-decreasing — past colors can only ever dim, never brighten, and only when a new record appears.

Experimental

EMA (smoothed trailing, α=0.1)
Exponentially-weighted moving average of per-snapshot max, then each column uses its EMA value. Smooths out the twitching that trailing-max modes have. Good for cleaner visuals without the repaint behavior of Window modes.
Trailing p95 (5 min)
95th percentile of |values| over trailing 5 min, instead of max. Ignores single-snapshot outlier spikes that would otherwise dominate the scale. Makes the "typical" activity more legible.
Log-compressed (visible range)
Max over visible range, but values pass through sign(v) · log(1+|v|) / log(1+max) before mapping to color. Compresses the top end and expands the bottom end — useful when one huge wall would otherwise wash everything else out.
Z-score per snapshot (±3σ)
Cell colored by (v − mean) / (3 × std) within each snapshot, clipped to ±1. Highlights per-snapshot outliers — the "unusual" strikes relative to the rest of that moment's distribution. Absolute magnitude is lost; you're seeing statistical salience.

Manual

Fixed cap
You type the denominator (Cap field appears when selected). Never changes — good for A/B comparisons across sessions.

Palette

Color scheme for positive/negative values. GEX (green/red) is the convention; Electric, Solar, and Ice/Fire are aesthetic alternatives using equally-saturated complementary hues with black at zero.

Hi-contrast (white-tipped)
Both poles ramp toward near-white at peak. Peaks hit the highest luminance of any palette here, so big walls "burn brighter" against the black background and changes in the largest values are easier to spot. Sign carried by tint at sub-peak: yellow-white for +, pink-white for −.
Stepped (4 bands)
Quantizes |t| into 4 brightness bands (0.45, 0.65, 0.85, 1.0). Any non-zero value snaps to ≥45% brightness, so a wall popping into existence is loud rather than a soft fade-in. A wall crossing a band boundary produces a visible step, not a smooth shade, making magnitude changes easy to spot. Lime + / hot magenta −. Magnitude resolution is intentionally coarse — pair with a Trailing or Snapshot scale if you want band transitions to track local activity.

Gamma

Non-linearity of the color mapping. Brightness = vγ (after sign and palette compose). Picture a curve with v on the x-axis and rendered brightness on the y-axis: γ = 1 is the diagonal, γ > 1 sags below it (mid-tones darken — only big peaks show), γ < 1 bows above it (mid-tones brighten — small magnitudes pop).

2.5 — peaks only (extreme)
Aggressive contrast. Anything below ~50% magnitude vanishes. Use when one or two giant walls dominate and you want to see only them, with everything else as background.
2.0 — peaks only
Strong noise suppression. Mid-magnitude activity dims significantly; the largest walls remain at full intensity. Good for "where are the real walls today, ignore everything else."
1.5 — emphasize highs
Moderate contrast. Slight darkening of mids; peaks are clearly the brightest things on screen. Useful in noisy regimes where mid-magnitude flutter clutters the view.
1.0 — Linear
True proportional rendering. A cell at 50% of max is exactly half as bright as full saturation. Most honest, but near-zero cells look close to black.
0.7 — mild lift
Slight brightness lift for low values without destroying magnitude contrast. Often the best "general purpose" non-linear setting.
0.55 — old default
Original behavior pre-v0.6.x. Heavily compresses mid-to-high and lifts low values — punchy visual but flattens differences between large magnitudes.
0.4 — strong lift
Aggressive low-magnitude visibility. Tiny walls become clearly visible. Cost: large walls all look similarly saturated, so magnitude differences at the top compress.
0.3 — extreme lift
Almost binary "is there anything here?" view. Any non-zero value reads as bright. Use when hunting for fresh walls just appearing or fading detail near the noise floor.
Pair γ > 1 with the Hi-contrast palette for max-emphasis-on-peaks; pair γ < 1 with the Stepped palette for max-visibility-on-everything.

Blend

How each strike fills its vertical pixel footprint in the heatmap.

None (sharp)
Flat rectangles per strike. No interpolation between strikes. Maximum faithfulness; shows each strike as an isolated cell.
Additive
Each strike adds a Gaussian brightness contribution. Soft and glow-like; same-sign clusters reinforce into bright peaks. Can wash out small opposite-sign strikes wedged between large same-sign neighbors.
Max (sign-safe)
Winner-take-all per pixel. Each strike holds the pixels where its own weighted contribution is largest. Sign identity is preserved; opposite-sign neighbors cannot cancel small strikes.

Col px

Pixel width of each snapshot column. Higher = larger cells, fewer snapshots on screen. Shift+scroll over the heatmap does the same thing live.

2 / 4 / 6 / 8 / 12
Standard widths — one snapshot per column.
1 / 0.5 / 0.25 / 0.1
Sub-pixel widths. Multiple snapshots collapse into a single output column via max-abs per strike — the strongest signal at each strike per time-bin survives. Lets you see longer time ranges at the cost of within-bin temporal resolution.
1d
Fixed layout for exactly one full US regular trading session (6.5h at 1s polling = 23,400 snaps). The width never changes as the buffer grows — the rendered session fills more of the canvas as the day progresses. Useful for "always-on session view."
fit
Auto-sizes to fit the entire current buffer in one frame, whatever its length. Aggregation kicks in automatically as the buffer grows.

Strike px

Cap each strike's vertical band height in CSS pixels. Controls how cramped or spread-out the heatmap looks vertically.

fit
Each strike fills the full slot between itself and the next strike. The traditional view — continuous heat field, no gaps. Bands grow taller as you zoom in vertically.
2 / 3 / 4 / 6 / 8 / 12
Fixed band thickness regardless of zoom. Black space appears between strikes — a Bookmap-style ladder where every strike reads as its own discrete level. Smaller numbers = more separation.
The Gaussian blend kernel scales with this setting. A thin Strike px gets a tiny kernel, so even in Additive or Max blend mode, neighboring strikes won't bleed into each other.

Refresh

How often the browser polls the API. 1s is fine for State-tier data; lower-frequency if you want to reduce load.

Session Save / Load / Restore

SAVE
Downloads the current snapshot buffer as a JSON file (gexbot-TICKER-TIMESTAMP.json). Includes ticker, expiry, overlay, and all snapshots in memory.
LOAD
Opens a file picker, loads a previously saved session JSON. Pauses live polling, switches Ticker/Expiry/Overlay to match the file, replaces the buffer. Click LIVE to resume appending new snapshots from where the file ended.
RESTORE
Auto-saved local backup. Every snapshot is written to the browser's IndexedDB in the background, keyed by date + ticker + expiry + overlay. After a server restart or browser reload, this button re-enables itself with the count for today's session matching your current selection — click to repopulate the buffer. Sessions older than today are auto-purged on startup. Browser-local: closing the browser profile or clearing site data wipes it. Use SAVE for portable archives.
LOAD and RESTORE both replace the current buffer — SAVE first if you want a portable copy. All MaxCh detection modes (CUSUM presets and event detectors) work on restored data.

Level traces

M+ (green)
Strike with the largest positive GEX imbalance at each snapshot.
M− (red)
Strike with the largest negative GEX imbalance.
ZG (yellow dashed)
Zero-gamma line. Only populated in GEX overlay mode (Greek overlays do not return zero_gamma).

Each is drawn as a horizontal trace across the visible columns. When the level jumps between strikes mid-session, the run at the old level ends and a new one starts at the new level — there's no vertical connector drawn through the intermediate prices.

AUTO ★ — live setup detector

The AUTO ★ button in the toolbar enables a real-time level detector + setup composer. When ON, three things happen:

Persistent level lines
Top-N "sticky" strikes (those that have been M+ or M− for a long time during the session) render as faint dashed horizontal lines across the chart with a left-edge badge: ★★ 7270 · 18m. ★ counts hold-time tiers (★ ≥ 5m, ★★ ≥ 15m, ★★★ ≥ 30m). Stale levels (no recent touch) dim further.
Auto-annotations
When a setup fires, a labeled point annotation is auto-dropped at the trigger snapshot. Auto-annotations have a soft halo to distinguish them from manual notes. They persist in IDB and survive reloads. Right-click to delete one; ⌫ clears all (auto + manual) for the current key.
Toast notifications
A floating panel in the top-right of the chart shows the last few setups with timestamp, spot, hold-time, and confidence stars. Click a row to scroll the chart to that snapshot.

Two setup types

Rejection
Spot is approaching a sticky wall (within ~0.06% by default), Pulse fired near the wall in the last 45s, and the regime is Long γ (or transitional). Expectation: dealers fade the move → bounce/reject at the wall. Default color: orange.
Breakout
Spot just crossed a sticky wall, velocity is sustained in the breakout direction, Pulse fired near the wall, and the regime is Short γ (or transitional). Expectation: dealers chase → continuation through the level. Default color: blue.

Arrow convention — v0.8.4

Both rejection and breakout labels now show ↑ or ↓ indicating where price is expected to GO after the event. Rejection arrow disambiguated by spot's position relative to the wall:

reject 7335 ↓
Spot was below 7335 (wall acted as resistance) → rejection means price expected to fall back DOWN. Bearish.
reject 7335 ↑
Spot was above 7335 (wall acted as support) → rejection means price expected to bounce back UP. Bullish.
break 7270 ↑
Spot crossed 7270 going up; expected continuation upward.

★ MARKS toggle (v0.8.8)

Toggles visibility of auto-generated reject / break annotations on the chart. When OFF, the orange/blue setup dots and labels disappear, leaving only the spot trace, level lines, manual annotations, and toast/log. The fires themselves still happen (history is preserved, log still shows them, toast still pops, IDB still stores them) — just not rendered on the chart. Useful when reviewing structure or sharing screenshots without setup clutter. Persisted in localStorage; default ON.

Setup log (LOG button)

Opens a panel showing the last 50 setup fires in memory, including those filtered out by the current ★ tier (those rows are greyed). Each row: time, type, strike, arrow, spot at fire, velocity, hold-time, regime, Pulse magnitude. Click a row to scroll the chart to that snapshot. Auto-updates as new fires arrive while the panel is open.

Cooldown: per (type, strike) the same setup can't refire for 3-6 minutes (sensitivity-dependent). Prevents the same wall test from spamming the toast on every snapshot.

Sensitivity

high
Wide near-wall band (±0.10%), 60s pulse-recency window, lenient velocity threshold, 6m cooldown. More setups, more noise.
med — default
±0.06% near-wall, 45s pulse window, 10m cooldown. Balanced.
low
±0.04% near-wall, 30s pulse window, 0.02% velocity floor, 15m cooldown. Fewer setups, higher conviction.

★ tier filter

Independent of sensitivity. Sets the minimum wall hold-time tier for two things:

  1. Which sticky-level lines render on the chart
  2. Which setups can emit annotations / toast / chirp

Toggling the dropdown produces immediate visual change — sticky-level lines appear / disappear and the ★ rate in the stats bar adjusts.

★★★ — default
Only walls with ≥30 minutes of session hold time fire alerts. The most selective; highly likely to be a real key level for the day.
★★+
Walls with ≥15 minutes of hold. Includes some emerging structure, more fires.
★+
Walls with ≥5 minutes. Most permissive; useful in the first hour of the session before any wall has reached ★★★, or for studying the live composer's full output.

Use the ★ rate readout in the stats bar to see live fires-per-hour after this filter applies. Color-coded: green < 5/h, yellow 5-15/h, red > 15/h.

Audio chirp (🔊 checkbox): brief WebAudio tone on each fire passing the tier filter (660 Hz rejection / 880 Hz breakout). Off by default. Some browsers block audio until the user interacts with the page.

MaxCh overlay

The MaxCh selector overlays strikes absorbing GEX-imbalance flow, sourced from the API's /maxchange endpoint (works in every overlay mode, Greek included). The raw feed is noisy — six buckets (current/1m/5m/10m/15m/30m) each pick a winning strike every second, with adjacent strikes randomly trading places. Three filter families are available, each tuned for a different question.

Pulse — per-strike z-score (fast + strong, recommended default)

Each strike maintains its own EMA of |dir| (Roberts EMVar). When the current snap's magnitude exceeds zfire × σ above the strike's own baseline, it fires immediately — no multi-snap persistence required. Hold-and-exit hysteresis keeps the glyph on as long as z ≥ zkeep, otherwise a hold-timer counts down.

fast
Low threshold, short hold. Catches rapid wall changes the moment they break out. Some noise. (EMA=10, zfire=1.5, hold=3)
normal — default
Balanced. Fires on clean breakouts of moderate-or-greater magnitude; ignores random single-bucket noise. (EMA=20, zfire=2.0, hold=5)
strict
High threshold, long hold. Only the cleanest, largest breakouts. Quiet sessions. (EMA=30, zfire=3.0, hold=8)

Pulse is magnitude-aware: a strike that wins one bucket with a huge value will fire, where CUSUM (below) would filter it as "not persistent." This is the right detector for spotting fresh walls forming, sharp wall flips, and similar quick events.

Loud — magnitude rank (added v0.8.7)

Complements Pulse. Where Pulse asks "is this print unusually large for THIS strike?" (a change detector), Loud asks "is this print large in absolute SESSION terms?" (a rank detector). Use Loud when a strike has been moderately active and you want to catch when it absorbs a print that's genuinely big regardless of its history — e.g., a wall absorbing 60% of session-max magnitude.

Algorithm: fire when |dir| at a strike ≥ sessionMaxAbs × magPct. No EMA, no z-score, no per-strike adaptation. Per-strike cooldown prevents repeat-fire on a sustained big print.

loose
≥10% of session max. ~10 fires/hour on a typical SPX session. Catches even moderately-large prints. (magPct=0.10, hold=3, cooldown=30s)
normal
≥20% of session max. ~5 fires/hour. Balanced — catches genuinely significant prints without much noise. (magPct=0.20, hold=5, cooldown=60s)
strict
≥35% of session max. ~1.5 fires/hour. Only the most significant prints — usually 5-15 events per session. (magPct=0.35, hold=8, cooldown=90s)

Calibrated against the 2026-05-06 SPX session where a 7375 wall print at 63% of session-max preceded a price rise. All three presets catch that event; loud_normal + loud_strict catch it without other noise nearby.

When to use Pulse vs Loud: they're complementary. Pulse catches "this strike just woke up." Loud catches "this strike just put up a really big number." A strike that's been M- for an hour and just doubled its print magnitude → Pulse fires (z-score spike). A strike that's been M- for an hour and just put up a session-record print → both fire. A strike that's been M- and consistent at 60% magnitude → only Loud fires (Pulse's EMA absorbed the consistent baseline).

Legacy: CUSUM + hysteresis (demoted in v0.8.2)

CUSUM is demoted to a "Legacy" group at the bottom of the dropdown. Empirical analysis of recorded sessions showed every preset (loose → extreme) firing 83-99% of snapshots — i.e., always on, with lift ≈ 1.0 vs random. By design CUSUM counts bucket wins, not magnitudes, so it filters out fast events in favor of multi-second sustained dominance — but in practice, sustained dominance is the steady state of /maxchange, not the exception. Pulse (above) is the recommended default for both quick events and persistent activity.

The presets remain available for niche cases where you specifically want the persistent-flow view. Per-strike signed CUSUM (Page 1954) with hysteresis (Schmitt 1938).

Event detectors — burst / swarm / pump

Where Pulse fires per-strike and CUSUM fires per-strike-with-persistence, these fire on aggregate snapshot conditions — stay quiet in steady state, fire when something coordinated happens across many strikes at once.

burst
Velocity z-score across all firing strikes. Sums |dir| over each snapshot, tracks a sliding 60-snap mean and σ, fires when the current snap's total exceeds mean + 2.5σ. Glyphs render at strikes contributing ≥ 15% of the spike's total magnitude. Catches general activity surges anywhere in the chain. Quiet because it normalizes against recent baseline.
swarm
Cluster detector. Fires when ≥ 2 same-sign firing strikes sit within ±5 price points of each other in the same snapshot, each carrying ≥ 5% of session max-abs. Catches "sweep" patterns where flow lights up a row of adjacent strikes simultaneously — signature of aggressive directional trading. Quiet because random single-strike noise rarely clusters.
pump — most stringent
Strict combination: burst condition (z ≥ 2.0, lowered slightly since combined) AND swarm condition AND the cluster center is within ±0.5% of latest spot. Designed for genuine pump precursors — coordinated near-spot directional surges. Will fire least often; expect long quiet periods punctuated by a few clean events per session.

The four families answer different questions:

  • Pulse — "what just changed at this strike" — per-strike velocity (z-score)
  • Loud — "is this print big in session terms" — per-strike magnitude rank
  • Event detectors — "is something coordinated happening right now" — snapshot-aggregate
  • CUSUM (legacy) — "where is sustained flow building" — per-strike persistence on bucket-wins

They're mutually exclusive in the dropdown — pick one based on what you're watching for.

Glyph encoding: color = direction of imbalance change (green = call-side accumulation, red = put-side accumulation); size = magnitude of the most recent print at the strike, normalized over the visible window; opacity = same.

Reading the layer: a band of glyphs at one strike across many consecutive snapshots = real persistent flow. The hysteresis means the strike won't drop in/out frame-by-frame at the threshold boundary — it stays "on" once it crosses the entry bar, until activity decays well below it. Solid clusters lining up vertically with the brightest heatmap bands are the "where the action is" strikes.

Theory note: the CUSUM detector (Page 1954) is minimax-optimal for change detection subject to a false-alarm bound (Lorden 1971); hysteresis (Schmitt 1938) is the canonical display-layer debouncer. Picked over Bayesian online changepoint detection (Adams & MacKay 2007) and BVP-style multi-stream detectors (Xie & Siegmund 2013) for simplicity — both available as upgrades if needed. Wavelets and microstructure-noise filters (Aït-Sahalia / Mykland / Zhang) and VPIN solve adjacent but distinct problems. The event detectors are simpler statistics (running z-score, cluster count) chosen for responsiveness rather than minimax optimality.
